What is it made of?

Liquid light has been built in four kinds of matter. Each one teaches us something different about what light is allowed to be. Click a card to see that recipe in the scene.
the classic
Gallium arsenide at 5 K
2009 · Paris and Lecce
A chip of gallium arsenide, two perfect mirrors a wavelength apart, cooled in a cryostat. Where it all started. The cleanest physics, the most measured.
Amo et al., Nature Physics 5, 805 (2009)
the breakthrough
Organic semiconductor at 300 K
2017 · Lecce and Montréal
A small organic molecule (TDAF) instead of a semiconductor. The exciton is fifty times more tightly bound. The cryogenics go away. Liquid light walks out of the freezer.
Lerario et al., Nature Physics 13, 837 (2017)
the modern
Halide perovskite at 300 K
2022 · Singapore and Lecce
The same family of materials making next-generation solar cells. Solution-processable. Cheap to fabricate. Room temperature. The platform that might one day live in your phone.
Su et al., Nature Communications 13, 7437 (2022)
the rare
Atoms colder than space
parallel track · Technion, Israel
A million rubidium atoms cooled to a hundred nanokelvin, behaving as one quantum object. Not liquid light — its older cousin. This is where the analog black hole story began.
Steinhauer et al., Nature 569, 688 (2019)
